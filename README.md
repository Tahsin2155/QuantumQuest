

## Set - 1

### 1. What was Franz expected to be prepared with for school that day?

Franz was expected to know the rules of participles, as M. Hamel had announced he would question the class on them. Franz had not studied them at all

### 2. What did Franz notice that was unusual about the school that day?

* The school was unusually quiet instead of the usual bustle.
* M. Hamel wore his formal Sunday clothes (green coat, frilled shirt, embroidered black silk cap) worn only on inspection/prize days.
* Village elders (old Hauser, the former mayor, former postmaster, etc.) sat silently on the back benches, which were always empty otherwise.
* The overall atmosphere was solemn and strange.

### 3. What had been put up on the bulletin-board?

An order from Berlin that only German would be taught in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine from then on — effectively ending French language instruction.

## Set - 2

### 1. What changes did the order from Berlin cause in school that day?

It transformed an ordinary school day into a solemn farewell. M. Hamel announced it was the last French lesson. The entire atmosphere became grave and attentive — students, teacher, and villagers alike focused on their work with an intensity never seen before. M. Hamel taught with exceptional patience and devotion, giving lessons in grammar, writing, and history, as if trying to pass on everything he knew in a single day.

### 2. How did Franz's feelings about M. Hamel and school change?

Franz underwent a complete reversal:

* He had earlier feared M. Hamel's ruler and found school burdensome; now he felt deep respect and sympathy for him.
* His books, once heavy nuisances, felt like "old friends" he couldn't bear to lose.
* He deeply regretted wasting time and not learning French properly.
* He saw M. Hamel not as a cranky teacher but as a dedicated, tragic figure sacrificing his home and career.

# Understanding the Text

### 1. What shows that people suddenly realise how precious their language is? Why does this happen?

Several things show this realisation:

* The village elders come to attend the last lesson voluntarily — a silent act of mourning and respect.
* Old Hauser brings a worn primer and reads along, even weeping.
* Franz, who dreaded French class, suddenly finds the lesson clear and beautiful, hanging on every word.
* The children write their copies with intense focus; even the youngest don't look up when beetles fly in.

This happens because loss makes people value what they took for granted. The threat of permanently losing their language makes them understand it is tied to their identity, memory, and freedom. M. Hamel's words — that language is "the key to their prison" — crystallise this feeling.

---

### 2. "Will they make them sing in German, even the pigeons?" — What could this mean?

This line carries more than one layer of meaning:

* Literally, Franz is struck by the absurdity of the Prussian order — even nature, even the cooing of pigeons on the roof, feels French to him. He wonders whether the occupiers will try to change even that.
* Symbolically, it suggests that language is as natural and instinctive as a bird's call — it cannot truly be legislated or suppressed by political orders. You cannot command a people's soul the way you command a soldier.
* Ironically, it reflects the helplessness and quiet anger of a conquered people — if the Prussians can ban a language from classrooms, where does their control end?

# Talking about the Text

### 1. Historical examples of conquered peoples having their language suppressed:

* **Ireland under British rule**: The English actively discouraged the Irish language (Gaelic), and after centuries of colonial pressure and famine-driven emigration, Irish nearly died out as a spoken tongue. It survives today largely through deliberate revival efforts.
* **Native Americans in the United States**: Indigenous children were forcibly sent to boarding schools with the explicit policy of "kill the Indian, save the man" — their native languages were forbidden, and children were punished for speaking them.
* **Korea under Japanese occupation (1910–1945)**: Japan banned Korean from schools and public life, forcing Koreans to adopt Japanese names and speak Japanese — a direct parallel to the story's situation.
* **Algeria under French colonisation**: Ironically, France itself imposed French on Algerian Berber and Arabic speakers, suppressing indigenous languages in schools — the very kind of cultural erasure Daudet critiques in *The Last Lesson*.

---

### 2. Linguistic minorities in Indian cities — keeping language alive:

Linguistic minorities (e.g., Punjabis in Bangalore, Tamilians in Mumbai) typically face pressure to adopt the dominant regional language. Ways they keep their language alive:

* **Community schools and classes** teaching the mother tongue to children
* **Cultural associations, festivals, and media** (newspapers, radio, YouTube channels) in their language
* **Family practice** — consciously speaking the language at home
* **Literature and digital content** — writing, podcasting, and creating in the minority language
* **Religion** — many languages are preserved through religious texts and rituals

The challenge is balancing **integration** with the host culture while **preserving linguistic identity**.

---

### 3. Linguistic chauvinism:

Yes, pride in one's language can be taken too far. **Linguistic chauvinism** means an aggressive, exclusionary belief that one's language is superior to others, often leading to:

* Hostility toward speakers of other languages
* Demanding that others abandon their language in shared spaces
* Political movements that marginalise minorities (e.g., "sons of the soil" agitations)

Examples in India include agitations over official language status, or insistence that migrants must learn only the local language. There is a fine line between **healthy pride** (preserving and celebrating one's language) and **chauvinism** (using language as a weapon of exclusion). M. Hamel's love for French is the former; the Prussian imposition is a state-level version of the latter.
